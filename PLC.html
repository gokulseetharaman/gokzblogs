Introduction

A PROGRAMMABLE LOGIC CONTROLLER (PLC) is an industrial computer control system that constantly monitors the status of input devices and makes decisions based on a custom programme to control the status of output devices.
PLCs have revolutionized the way industrial processes are controlled. They have enabled the automation of complex tasks, improved efficiency and reduced costs.
PLCs remain an essential component of many industrial control systems. In fact, they remain the most widely used industrial control technology worldwide. The ability to work with PLCs is required for many different professions, ranging from engineers designing the system to electrical technicians who maintain it.

Function of PLC

The PLC receives data from connected sensors or input devices, processes it, and generates outputs based on predefined parameters.
A PLC, depending on its inputs and outputs, can monitor and record run-time data such as machine productivity or operating temperature, start and stop processes automatically, generate alarms if a machine malfunctions, and so on. Programmable Logic Controllers are a versatile and reliable control solution that can be applied to almost any application.


Types

Compact PLC: Compact PLCs are small in size and usually have a limited number of I/O points. They are designed with space as a key consideration, and the control requirements are simple. Compact PLCs are commonly used in small-scale control applications where the number of devices to control and monitor is limited. Simple machines, stand-alone equipment, and simple processes are all examples.
      Example:Siemens SIMATIC S7-1200.

Modular PLC: Modular PLCs use a modular architecture in which the CPU, power supply, and input-output modules are all separate components. This modular design allows for greater flexibility in system configuration, making it easier to expand or modify the system in response to changing requirements. Modular PLCs are ideal for applications that will grow or change over time. Industries with changing control requirements, such as manufacturing plants that may add production lines.
      Example: Allen-Bradley ControlLogix.

Rack PLC: This is a type of High end PLC which is specially designed for complex and demanding application. It has maximum number of I/O points also Rack-mount PLCs are modular and can easily expanded by adding additional modules to the rack.
      Example:Schneider Electric Modicon Quantum.


Programming Languages for PLC

As specified by the International Electrotechnical Commission (IEC) 61131, five programming languages are used to code PLCs:

Ladder Logic: Ladder logic is also known as ladder diagrams. It is essentially a graphical representation of a relay logic circuit, consisting of two vertical parallel lines joined by horizontal lines. These lines are known as rails and rungs. The vertical line supplies power while also creating logic.

Functional Block Diagram(FBD): The Function Block Diagram (FBD) in PLC programming is a graphical representation of control logic. It employs function blocks to depict operations such as comparisons or timers, with inputs and outputs represented by lines. Contacts and coils represent input conditions and output actions. Branches and junctions regulate logic flow.

Structured Text: A high-level textual language that promotes structured programming. It has a language structure (syntax) that closely resembles PASCAL and supports a wide range of standard functions and operators.

Instruction List: A low-level "assembler-like" language based on instructions found in many modern PLCs.

Sequential Function chart: A method for programming complex control systems on a more structured level. A SFC programme is an overview of the control system, with entire programme files serving as the foundation. Each programme file is written in one of the other types of programming languages. The SFC approach coordniates large, complex programming tasks into smaller, more manageable ones.


Advantages

Flexibility - Easily programmable to perform different functions.
Reliability - High durability and reliability in harsh industrial environments.
Scalability - Modular design allows for easy expansion and upgrades.
Real-Time Operation - Capable of real-time processing for control applications.
Ease of Troubleshooting - Diagnostic tools help identify issues quickly.
Integration - Can be integrated with other industrial systems like SCADA and HMI.
Maintenance - Low maintenance due to solid-state components and built-in diagnostics.
Data Handling - Capable of handling complex data processing and communication.
Energy Efficiency - Energy-efficient designs reduce operational costs.
User-Friendly Programming - Programming languages like Ladder Logic are intuitive for engineers.

Disadvantages

Initial Cost - High initial investment compared to relay-based systems.
Complexity - Can be complex to program and configure for beginners.
Limited Processing Power - May not be suitable for extremely high-speed or computationally intensive applications.
Vendor Lock-In - Proprietary software and hardware can lead to vendor lock-in.
Learning Curve - Requires specialized training and knowledge for programming and maintenance.
Size Constraints - May be too bulky for very small or space-constrained applications.
Limited Memory - Memory limitations may restrict the complexity of programs.
Environmental Sensitivity - Although rugged, extreme environmental conditions can still affect performance.
Response Time - Slower response time compared to some real-time computing systems.
Upgrading Issues - Upgrading or replacing PLCs can be costly and time-consuming.


Application

PLCs are widely used in various applications such as:
   ⦿Manufacturing Automation - Controlling assembly lines, robotic arms, and machinery in factories.
   ⦿Process Control - Managing processes in industries like chemical, pharmaceutical, and food processing.
   ⦿Building Automation - HVAC control, lighting systems, and security systems in buildings.
   ⦿Energy Management - Controlling power distribution and renewable energy systems.
   ⦿Water Treatment - Monitoring and controlling water treatment plants and sewage systems.
   ⦿Automotive Industry - Automation of manufacturing processes and quality control in automotive plants.
   ⦿Material Handling - Conveyor systems, sorting machines, and automated storage and retrieval systems.
   ⦿Oil and Gas - Monitoring and controlling drilling operations, pipelines, and refineries.
   ⦿Telecommunications - Managing and controlling telecommunications infrastructure and networks.
   ⦿Transportation - Traffic light control systems, railway signaling, and airport baggage handling systems.
