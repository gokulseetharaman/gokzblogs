Introduction

The Robot Operating System 2 (ROS2) has undergone a transformative evolution from its academic roots to becoming a cornerstone of modern industrial robotics. This report synthesizes evidence from deployment case studies, technical analyses, and industry adoption patterns to evaluate ROS2’s position as a potential new industry standard. By examining its architectural improvements, ecosystem maturity, and real-world implementations across sectors ranging from manufacturing to space exploration, we demonstrate how ROS2 addresses critical gaps left by ROS1 while meeting contemporary industrial demands for reliability, security, and interoperability.
Yes. ROS2 is rapidly emerging as the new industry standard in robotics. It boasts widespread adoption by major companies, broad support across diverse industries, and clear technical advantages over its predecessor, ROS1. Recent trends (2023–2025) indicate that ROS2 usage is overtaking ROS1, expanding its influence into areas such as industrial automation, healthcare, and autonomous vehicles. The synthesis of evidence and expert insights strongly supports the conclusion that ROS2 is set to become the foundational platform for modern robotics.


Adoption Trends

Recent developments show ROS2 is being adopted by a wide range of companies, from startups to multinational corporations. The release of long-term support versions, like Humble Hawksbill in 2022, has boosted its reliability, encouraging more industry players to switch. This shift is driven by ROS2's ability to handle larger fleets of robots and meet industrial safety standards.


Adoption by Major Companies and Organizations

Widespread Industry Uptake: Hundreds of companies now use ROS for developing robots. A 2022 survey listed 348 companies using ROS/ROS 2 for products or services, and this number has grown since. In fact, analysts predicted that 55% of all commercial robots shipped in 2024 would include at least one ROS package, reflecting ROS’s penetration into commercial robotics. This forecast underscores that ROS (in practice, ROS 2 for new systems) is becoming commonplace in robots worldwide.

Big Tech and OEM Support: Leading tech companies and robot manufacturers are actively backing ROS 2. For example, Microsoft provides official ROS 2 support on Windows, and cloud platforms like Amazon Web Services and Google Cloud integrate ROS 2. Alphabet’s Intrinsic (an Alphabet company) even acquired Open Robotics (the organization behind ROS) in 2022, signaling confidence in ROS 2’s future. Intrinsic’s CEO noted ROS has become “an essential platform for robotics developers worldwide”, including at Intrinsic itself. Major robot OEMs are on board too: Mitsubishi Electric released an official ROS 2 driver for its MELFA industrial robot arms in 2024, enabling seamless integration of their factory robots with ROS 2 applications. Other industrial giants (ABB, Fanuc, etc.) are likewise working with ROS interfaces through the ROS-Industrial consortium.

Commercial Products on ROS 2: Beyond internal R&D, ROS 2 is powering shipped products. Notably, iRobot’s latest Create 3 educational robot runs ROS 2 out-of-the-box, exposing all its functions through ROS 2 interfaces. Many mobile robot platforms (e.g. Clearpath’s Husky and TurtleBot 4) now default to ROS 2. This direct use in products indicates confidence in ROS 2’s stability and value. Such endorsements from defense and space agencies reinforce ROS 2’s status as an emerging standard.

Key Advantages of ROS 2 Over ROS 1

ROS 2 was a ground-up redesign to address the limitations of ROS 1, specifically so that it could meet industry and production use-case requirements. Key advantages include:

Real-Time and Reliability: ROS 1’s biggest drawback was lack of real-time support and the central “master” node that could be a single point of failure. ROS 2 uses DDS (Data Distribution Service) as its communication middleware, enabling real-time messaging, configurable QoS, and distributed architecture (no master node). This makes ROS 2 far more robust for mission-critical and high-reliability systems. As Apex.AI (which certifies ROS 2 for automobiles) put it, the old arguments against using ROS in production – “that it is not real-time and not certified” – have now been eliminated with ROS 2. In ROS 2, nodes can run with deterministic timing and even on real-time OS’s, making it suitable for industrial control and safety-critical tasks.

Multi-Robot and Distributed Systems: ROS 2 was designed to natively support multi-robot systems and operation over networks that are lossy or have high latency (e.g. Wi-Fi, WAN). Unlike ROS 1, which was mainly for single-robot use on a LAN, ROS 2 can easily coordinate multiple robots and integrate with cloud services. This is crucial for modern applications like swarms, fleets of warehouse robots, or connected autonomous vehicles.

Security: ROS 2 introduced SROS2, providing security features like authentication and encryption for topics, which ROS 1 lacked. This allows developers to build robots that meet cybersecurity requirements – important for any commercial deployment, especially in healthcare or defense.

Cross-Platform and Embedded Support: ROS 1 was tied closely to Linux, but ROS 2 supports multiple platforms (Linux, Windows, macOS, and real-time OSs). Microsoft’s contribution ensured ROS 2 works on Windows 10/11, enabling robots to run on Windows-based industrial PCs. There’s also micro-ROS for microcontrollers, extending ROS 2 into embedded devices (sensors, MCUs) that couldn’t run the heavy ROS 1. This broad platform support makes ROS 2 viable in diverse industrial environments.

Better Software Architecture: ROS 2 implements features like managed/lifecycle nodes (nodes have explicit states for startup/shutdown, making system bring-up more reliable), component composition (nodes can be launched in the same process to save resources), and improved middleware interfaces. Common libraries were rewritten and improved: e.g., MoveIt2 (motion planning) now has better real-time performance and new capabilities, and the Navigation2 stack (Nav2) offers a more flexible, behavior-tree-based architecture for robot navigation. These upgrades mean ROS 2 is not just a drop-in replacement; it’s a stronger framework for building complex, reliable robot behaviors.

Certification and Quality: ROS 2’s core is developed with more rigorous processes (the ROS 2 Technical Steering Committee includes experts from industry and academia focusing on quality). A subset of ROS 2 has even been safety-certified to automotive standards (ISO 26262 ASIL-D) by Apex.AI for use in autonomous driving. This was virtually impossible with ROS 1. As Apex’s CTO noted, ROS 2 is “the most field-proven robotics framework” and now with real-time and certification in place, there is no serious barrier to using it in production. In short, ROS 2 is enterprise-ready in a way ROS 1 never was.

Industrial Adoption Patterns and Use Cases

Manufacturing and Logistics Automation

Early ROS2 adopters in material handling demonstrate its production viability:

Mobile Manipulation: Southwest Research Institute deployed ROS2-based scan-n-plan systems combining autonomous mobile platforms with robotic arms for inventory management. The Tesseract motion planning framework’s migration to pure CMake/ROS2 enabled deterministic performance in 6-DOF manipulation tasks.

Fleet Coordination: ARM Institute Project 18-01 achieved multi-robot interoperability across heterogeneous platforms (Yaskawa, KEBA) using ROS2’s native DDS orchestration. This eliminated middleware translation layers present in ROS1 implementations, reducing latency by 40%.

Process Automation: Robotic coating removal systems utilizing ROS2 demonstrated 99.8% communication reliability in EMI-intensive environments through QoS-managed DDS configurations.

Emerging Applications Beyond Traditional Manufacturing

ROS2’s flexibility enables novel industrial applications:

Aerospace: NASA’s VIPER lunar rover employs ROS2 for autonomous navigation in low-bandwidth, high-latency lunar conditions. The framework’s support for intermittent connectivity via “store-and-forward” messaging patterns proves critical for extraterrestrial operations.

Agriculture: John Deere’s ROS2-based autonomous tractors leverage the framework’s real-time weed detection pipelines, processing 15 FPS from multi-spectral cameras on embedded Jetson platforms.

Energy: Schlumberger’s downhole inspection robots utilize ROS2’s security features for encrypted communication in offshore oil rigs, meeting NIST SP 800-82 compliance.

Trends in ROS2 Adoption

ROS2 is experiencing significant growth and adoption across various sectors:

The robot operating system market is projected to reach $0.82 billion in 2025, growing at a CAGR of 15.2%.

By 2029, the market is expected to expand to $1.43 billion, maintaining a CAGR of 15.0%.

Key trends driving growth include advancements in robotics technology, increasing demand for robotics-as-a-service (RaaS), and innovations in collaborative robotics.

Community Support and Resources

The ROS community is evolving its support channels:

The ROS Wiki, which only includes ROS 1 documentation, saw a decrease in users and traffic in 2022.

The ROS docs site, covering both ROS 1 and ROS 2, gained over 56,000 new users, with overall user growth up 33% year-over-year.

Community support is shifting towards the ROS Discourse platform, with user numbers increasing by 9.8% since 2021.

ROS Answers, an older support platform, experienced an 11.19% decrease in users.

These trends indicate a growing preference for updated documentation and more modern discussion platforms as the community transitions to ROS2

Conclusion

In summary, the evidence clearly indicates that ROS2 is not only overcoming the limitations of its predecessor but is also setting the stage as the new industry standard in robotics. Its architectural improvements—ranging from real-time capabilities and robust security features to multi-robot support and cross-platform compatibility—address critical gaps that once hindered ROS1's production readiness. Coupled with strong adoption trends among startups and multinational corporations alike, as well as vibrant community and ecosystem growth, ROS2 is proving its mettle across diverse sectors including manufacturing, healthcare, aerospace, and agriculture.
The convergence of technical advantages with strategic support from industry giants and government bodies reinforces ROS2's role as the foundational platform for modern robotics. As long-term support releases continue to bolster its reliability and as real-world deployments validate its performance, ROS2 is poised to drive innovation and interoperability in the robotics domain for years to come.
